1 INTRODUCTION 
The OKRV Style Guide is a standard baseline. It is not intended as a ‘must-do’ manual for the seasoned OKRV Production Associate on creating art or integrating proposal products. 
The purpose of this Style Guide is to show various examples of minimum standards for the appearance and style of documents we produce, and our incremental products for developing a professional looking proposal. 
1.1 STYLE GUIDE ORGANIZATION 
The Style Guide includes the following sections: 
2 – Proposal Layout and Design 
– discusses preface material and the body of a proposal document 
3 – Styles for Documents 
– offers guidelines for text, header and footer, body, and figure caption styles, as well as guidelines for custom toolbar development 
4 – Proposal Figures 
– covers placement of figures and their callouts, and includes an overview   
5 – Artwork Development 
– deals with text styles for artwork, sizing standards, and template design standards 
6 – Final Production Media 
– offers guidelines for cover and CD artwork, tabs, and shipping materials 
7 – Writing Style 
– is a basic proposal grammar reference guide

2.1 TITLE PAGES 
Title pages must be compliant with the RFP. They may be similar to the cover, or simple in appearance. At a minimum, include the title of the document, submission date, solicitation number, client name and contact information, intended customer, and complete copyright or proprietary notice text. Note that some of this information may need to be repeated on the document cover. 
2.2 TABLE OF CONTENTS AND OTHER LISTS 
Proposals must include tables of contents, which must be compliant with the RFP. Generate a table of contents from the headings within the body of the proposal. 
Include heading levels deep enough to make it easy for the evaluator to locate a particular section. 
The style of a table of contents should be consistent with the proposal template. 
If not requested in the RFP, a list of figures is optional. The style of a list of figures should be consistent with the proposal template.

2.4 COPYRIGHT NOTICE 
As a minimum, include the ‘© yyyy Client Name’ statement in the footer of all proposal volumes, along with any nondisclosure statement and/or required security markings. 
2.5 APPENDICES 
Generally, appendices are denoted A, B, C, etc. The document identification and the relevant appendix identifier should show in the appendix footer. 

Sometimes, appendices are required to be within the main document, i.e. share a common page numbering scheme. Prefix the appendix identifier of these paragraphs, e.g. A.1. However, Word does not allow two different styles of numbering within the same document, even if they are in different sections. 
Therefore, add the headings for the appendix manually. The Appendix 1, 2, 3 and 
4 styles mimic the Heading 1, etc., styles, except that they have no numbering. Using an Appendix 1 style works well in this case. 
2.5.1 Landscape Pages 
Landscape (rotated) pages are discouraged in OKRV proposals. Occasionally, a graphic or table is more suited to a landscape page rather than a portrait page. 
Where possible, rotate the graphic insertion, and leave the portrait page with its header and footer intact. If necessary, insert a landscape page in the middle of a portrait-orientated document (or vice-versa) by inserting section breaks before and after, and then decoupling the headers and footers for each of the sections. 
Depending on the software used to create the artwork, it may be better to rotate the artwork before insertion into the Word document. 
2.6 ARTWORK INTEGRATION 
Generally, figures appear in the corners of the pages, in the first available corner following the callout to that figure in the text. In other words, if a callout is at the bottom of the right column, in a two-column template, then place the figure in the upper left corner of the next page. This rule applies as well for full-width figures, which go in the next available corner/space, at either the upper half or lower half of the page, following the callout to that figure in the text. It is acceptable to place a full-width figure at the lower half of a page where the callout first appears in the second column. 
2.6.1 Figures 
There is no specific application that is better than another for creating proposal graphics. Products such as Microsoft® PowerPoint®,  Adobe® Illustrator® or PhotoShop®, can all be used. 
 
The optimum format for inserting a graphic into a proposal is to Paste Special as a Picture (Enhanced Metafile). Alternatively, paste special the graphic as a Picture (Windows Metafile), as a JPEG bitmap or as a GIF bitmap. 
Never create artwork using the drawing tools built in to Microsoft Word. 
2.6.2 Tables 
In proposal modules, tables are classified as artwork figures. Tables can be created in separate Word documents, or in PowerPoint. Excel tables should first be exported to a separate Word document, or to PowerPoint prior to insertion into the proposal module.

3.2 HEADERS AND FOOTERS 
Headers and footers identify the document, our client, explicit section and/or page identification, and identification of the intended party/customer. They normally are set up outside any format margin requirements for proposal content. That is to say that a 1” margin requirement on all sides of each page does not exclude using that margin for headers and footer. 
 
Headers and footers must be compliant with the RFP. As a guideline, place the client logo in the header, flush with the left margin. Place the title of the document, and any RFP required information, to the right. Place the delivery date, copyright information, and section and/or page numbering in the footer. 
Headers and footers can vary in appearance, and most are acceptable, as long as the layout is compliant. 
 
Guidelines and Tips for Headers and Footers  
¦ Include all necessary information for headers and footers to be compliant 

¦ Use correct security, copyright and trademark markings 

¦ In Page Setup, use a minimum 0.2” ‘From edge’ setting 

¦ It is acceptable to use a ‘work-in-progress’ header and footer during proposal development, time permitting, and to insert the final quality header and footer during final production 

¦ Follow RFP instructions for pagination at a minimum 

¦ Keep pagination simple, but unambiguous, so that if the customer takes apart the sections for evaluation, reassembly is easy 

¦ Set headers and footers in the margin, outside the content space 

¦ Creativity is fine, but artwork and text in the content should attract the evaluator’s attention 

¦ Use a graphics application to properly resize artwork, to minimize the file size for artwork as much as possible 


3.2.1 Identity Artwork 
Use artwork in the headers and footers only where that artwork quality is high. Clean up pixilation, blurring, discoloration or fading. 
 
Guidelines and Tips for Identity Artwork

3.4 BODY TEXT 
Follow the RFP format instructions to determine the sequence for headings 
Use the built-in Word heading styles to format headings, and automatically number headings with the numbering format requirement 
Use hidden text to force automatic numbering at the start of a module Heading levels should correlate to those in the RFP 
Use unnumbered headings for topics within sections which do not correlate to heading numbering in the RFP 
Preferably, use a sans serif font/typeface for headings, e.g. Arial 
Never leave one widow or orphan line at the end or at the top of a column 
‘Normal’ is the style on which all others (unless based on “No style”) are based. If the font or paragraph formatting of the Normal style changes, it will ripple through most other document styles. It is better to use other styles intended for specific purposes when building structured proposal documents. 
The ‘Body Text’ style is intended for the body of a proposal document. By default it is identical to ‘Normal’ except for having 6-point spacing after, i.e. each Body Text paragraph is followed by half a vertical line of space. For more or less space, a first-line indent, a different font or any other change, customize the Body Text style without affecting other styles except for those in the Body Text family, i.e. the ‘Theme’, ‘Bullet 1’ and ‘Bullet 2’ styles. 
 
Guidelines and Tips for Body Text  
¦ In a page-limited proposal, set the points before and after paragraphs to zero, use a first-line, and the minimum allowable font size 

¦ In a proposal without page limits, use at least 3 points of space before and after paragraphs 

¦ Follow the RFP instructions for minimum font and/or line spacing point size 

¦ Preferably, use Arial or Times New Roman for the Body Text style, since these fonts are standard to Microsoft Windows 

¦ Use a single space between sentences, and do not use blank lines between text paragraphs 

¦ Never leave one widow or orphan line at the end or at the top of a column 

¦ “Bottom out” or balance columns on the last page of major sections, during final production

The Theme style is for theme statements made at the beginning of a module, following the Section title (Heading). Generally, it is an italicized version of the Body text style. Use of an alternate text color is optional. 
 
 
Guidelines and Tips for Theme styles  
¦ In a page limited proposal, use the minimum allowable font size 

¦ Italicize the Theme style 

¦ Base the Theme style on Body text 

¦ With management approval, use an alternate color for the Theme style 


3.4.2 Bullet 1 and Bullet 2 
¦ The ‘Bullet 1’ style is flush with the left text margin in the template, with a hanging indent for text 

¦ Bullet 1 style is based on the Body Text style ¦ ‘Bullet 2’ is flush left with the start of the Bullet 1 style text 

¦ The bullet character or symbol is the bullet line punctuation. Therefore, no period is required, unless the bulleted item contains more than one complete sentence

Adjust column widths, and use adjacent cells with invisible inside borders for bullet lists, to minimize the height of the table.

The following section is a useful guide to punctuation, spelling and the use of the English language. Examples are shown in a sans-serif font. For more complex writing issues, you should invest in a Chicago or Franklin style guide. 
7.1 PUNCTUATION 
The purpose of punctuation is to make the meaning of the text as clear as possible. Incorrect or insufficient punctuation can cause ambiguity; unnecessary punctuation may be unsightly. The modern tendency is to use punctuation sparingly. However, providing it is used correctly, respect the author’s preferences.

7.1.2 Colon 
At the end of a title or subtitle 
After captions which are incomplete sentences. A caption comprising an incomplete sentence and a complete sentence requires a period after each sentence. 
After dates or signatures 
Within or after abbreviations consisting of one or more capital letter, or after contractions 
After the symbols for units of measurement or currency 
Use a colon to mark a pause or degree of separation shorter than a period. Use a colon, also, to introduce a run-in list or one set off from the text, for example: 

The receiver comprises the following modules:

A colon is also used to indicate a ratio, e.g. VSWR of 1.3:1. Note that no spaces are used before or after the colon. 
7.1.3 Semicolon 
A semicolon separates parts of a sentence that require a stronger break than a comma, but are too closely related to be broken into two separate sentences. 
Semicolons should be used to precede clauses introduced by words such as 
however, nevertheless, thus, accordingly, therefore, that is and namely.

The comma is used in a wide variety of different situations that are too numerous to list in this document; however, commas should not be used: 
¦ After such abbreviations as i.e., e.g. and viz. 

¦ In addresses 

¦ In dates 

¦ Before text in parentheses 

¦ Before or after text in dashes 


7.1.5 Question Mark 
Although the use of a question mark it is not common within proposal text, a few simple do’s and don’ts are listed below: 
¦ The question mark is used to show text that represents a direct question, a rhetorical or unspoken question or indicates doubt. 

¦ The question mark should not be used to show text that represents an indirect question, a statement of fact that includes words of interrogation or after single interrogative words in a sentence. 


7.1.6 Exclamation Point 
Generally, do not use an exclamation mark within proposal text. 
7.1.7 Dashes 
A dash may refer to several different marks. Their typographical representation and use is outlined below. 
7.1.7.1 Dash (Em Rule) 





This is represented by —, and is obtained by Ctrl+Alt+Num-. It is used instead of parentheses within a sentence, but note that only one set of em rules should be used within a single sentence; parentheses should be used for two or more parenthetical elements. 
7.1.7.2 En rule 





The en rule is represented by –, and is obtained by Ctrl+Num-. Although a hyphen (see para. 7.1.7.3) will generally suffice, an en rule should be used: 
¦ In spans of figures, e.g. pages 45–7, 1996–7 financial year. It should not be used where figures could have negative quantities, e.g. -5 to +10dBm, not 






-5–+10dBm 
¦ To express association between words which retain their separate identity, e.g. 






Thomson–Marconi joint venture 
A spaced en rule ( – ) should be used where the unspaced en rule may cause the text to be reread, e.g. Thomson – GEC–Marconi team. 
7.1.7.3 Hyphen 





This is the most commonly used dash and its uses are to numerous to list here. Note that, in common with mot modern word processing software, Microsoft® Word has three different hyphens available: 
¦ The normal hyphen, obtained by - 

¦ The hard, or non-breaking, hyphen, obtained by Ctrl+Shift+-. This is used if you do not want the hyphenated word to break over the end of a line. It must also be used when a minus sign is required to stop the minus sign and its associated number becoming separated at the end of a line, e.g. -10 dBm 

¦ The soft hyphen, obtained by Ctrl+-. This can be inserted to break long words at the end of line to avoid excessive white space in the preceding line. However, its general use must be carefully considered 






7.1.8 Parentheses 
Parentheses, i.e. ( and ), are used to enclose expressions that are not essential to the meaning of the sentence but that amplify or clarify. Note the following: 
¦ When a parenthesis within a parenthesis is required, two pairs of parentheses should be used, not a combination of parentheses, square brackets and/or ‘curly’ brackets. Alternatively, a combination of dashes and parentheses could be used 

¦ The punctuation within parentheses is governed by the grammatical demands of the enclosed expression 

¦ A comma follows closing parentheses only if a comma would have been used if there had been no parentheses 

¦ A complete sentence within parentheses has its punctuation mark within the parentheses 


7.1.9 Square Brackets 
Originally used to denote words or phrases inserted into the text by someone other than the author, the use of square brackets, i.e. [ and ], should be reserved for such expressions as [continued overleaf] and [Turn to page 44]. 
7.1.10 Points of Ellipsis 
The three points of ellipsis (…), obtained by Alt+Ctrl+., are principally used to mark omission of words in quoted matter, e.g. “Protective statements…ensure the protection of Company Intellectual Property Rights…” No punctuation mark should precede or follow the three points. 
7.1.11 Apostrophe 
The principal use of the apostrophe, normally preceded or followed by s is to denote possession. It is only used with nouns. The pronouns his, its, etc., are already possessive and do not need the apostrophe. Note that it’s means it is, while its means belonging to it. 
The possessive is formed for any singular or plural noun that ends in any letter other than s by adding apostrophe-s, e.g. a child’s toy. Plural nouns ending in s are made possessive by adding an apostrophe, e.g. the Smiths’ house. Singular nouns ending in s take apostrophe-s, e.g. Dickens’s novels, unless the word is either biblical or ancient. 
A distinction is sometimes made between animate and inanimate nouns, e.g. the person’s legs but the legs of the table rather than the table’s legs. 
When the sense of a noun is adjectival rather than possessive, the apostrophe-s is frequently omitted, e.g. travelers checks, since this would be rephrased with the preposition for rather than of, i.e. checks for travelers. 
The apostrophe is omitted in expressions of time, e.g. in two months time. Place names are also spelt without the apostrophe, e.g. St Johns Church. 
An apostrophe is also used to indicate the omission of letters, e.g. don’t means do not. However, the use of the shortened forms such as this is not recommended in proposal text. The apostrophe can also prevent confusion, e.g. dot the i’s and cross the t’s. 
7.1.12 Solidus 
The solidus (/), also known as a virgule or slash, is used to: 
¦ indicate alternative, e.g. yes/no 

¦ to form standard abbreviations, e.g. a/c 

¦ to denote a fraction, e.g. 1/3 

¦ to express per, e.g. 20 km/h 


and should not be used instead of other punctuation marks. 
7.1.13 Quotation Marks 
The principal use of quotation marks is to enclose the exact words of a writer or speaker, whether or not these form a complete sentence or sentences. Single quotation marks should be used. Double quotation marks are only used for quotations within quotations. 
7.1.14 Typography of Punctuation 
Print punctuation marks in the same style as the preceding word or phrase if they belong to that word or phrase. If they relate to the text as a whole, they are set in the style of the text. For example, A, B, C and D are related (see 10a). 
7.2 SHORTENED FORMS 
 
7.2.1 Definitions 
There are four shortened forms of words. 

7.2.1.1 Abbreviation 





An abbreviation is a shortened form of a word or words that consists only of the initial letter, or of the initial letter and other letters, but not the final letter. 
Abbreviations written with lower-case letters only or with an initial capital are given full stops: 

Mon. Jan. vol. p. op. cit. M. i.e. e.g. 
Abbreviations that consist of more than one capital letter or of capital letters only, are written without full stops: 
ASPCA PhD USA DoD ISBN 
7.2.1.2 Contractions 





A contraction is a shortened form that consists of at least the first and last letters of a word. It is written without a full stop: 
Dept vols Ltd Govt 
7.2.1.3 Acronyms 





An acronym is a shortened form that is always pronounced as a word. It may be formed from the initial letters of other words: 
¦ Nato (from North Atlantic Treaty Organization) 






or from a number of letters belonging to the phrase being shortened: 
¦ sonar (sound navigation and ranging) 






Acronyms are written without periods. 
7.2.1.4 Symbols 





A symbol is an internationally recognized representation of a unit of measurement or of a concept—not an abbreviation and, as such, written without a period: 
km (kilometer) A (ampere) g (gram) Hz (Hertz) p (probability) 
7.2.2 Use 
Shortened forms save space and make reading easier by eliminating needless repetition. They should only be used with core words that by repetition in the text become familiar to the reader. A shortened form should never be used only once in the text. 
Some contractions, abbreviations and symbols are conventionally used without explanation, e.g. Mr.; Dr.; a.m.; p.m.; kg; and Ltd. Notes, tables and figures traditionally use shortened forms to save space. 
Other shortened forms should be presented at the earliest possible opportunity in the text, but not in a preface or other preliminary matter. The name, term or expression should be given in full at the first mention, followed by the shortened form in parentheses, e.g. Steven Myers & Associates (OKRV). 
Consult the proposal manager or volume lead when two expressions have the same shortened form. 
7.2.3 Acronyms 
Acronyms formed from phrases written with lower-case letters throughout do not take initial capitals, e.g. radar (radio detection and ranging). Acronyms formed from the names of organizations written with initial capitals may retain the initial capital only, e.g. Nato. When the use of lower-case letters in acronyms would give rise to confusion, or when the acronyms are less well known, capitals should be used, e.g. WHO (World Health Organization). 
7.2.4 Addresses 
Addresses should be spelt out in full, except for references to post office boxes, which should be of the form PO Box 1. 
7.2.5 Ampersand 
The ampersand (&) is a shortened form of and. The use of the ampersand should be restricted to tables and captions where space is at a premium. It should also be used where it is normally used by a third party, e.g. P&O, being the shortened form used by the Peninsular and Oriental Steam Navigation Company. 
7.2.6 Apostrophe 
The apostrophe can be used with shortened forms in the normal manner to indicate possession, e.g. Nato’s policy… The apostrophe is always required in contractions such as can’t, but the use of these contractions is not recommended in proposal text. It is not required for every omission, e.g. Dept, not Dep’t. Common shortened forms are rarely written with an apostrophe, e.g. phone. 
7.2.7 Beginning a Sentence 
Providing that the full name has been given earlier, acronyms, etc., may be used at the beginning of a sentence, e.g. Nato proposals… In body text, the full forms of vol., fig., etc., should always be used at the beginning of a sentence, e.g. Figure 3 shows…, not Fig. 3 shows… The abbreviated forms may be used, with a lower- case initial, at the beginning of notes, etc., that are not full sentences, e.g. vol. 3. 
7.2.8 Capitals 
An initial capital should always be used for an abbreviation or contraction if the word normally bears an initial capital. Conversely, a word that is used only in its shortened form, and that begins with a lower-case letter, does not take an initial capital, even at the beginning of a note, e.g. cf. fig. 3. 
Sometimes, capitals are required for abbreviations that do not ordinarily have an initial capital, e.g. TV (television). Acronyms may only need an initial capital if well known (Nato), capitals throughout if less well known or confusing (WHO) or no capitals at all (radar) except when used to begin a sentence. 
7.2.9 Compass Points 
In tables and figures, each point of the compass is abbreviated to its first letter or letters, e.g. N, SSE. Compass points should be spelt out in general text, e.g. At latitude 22º12’30” south… not lat. 22º15’30” S… 
References to geographical areas should be spelt out in general text (south east Asia) but may be abbreviated in tables and figures (SE Asia). 
7.2.10 Days, Months and Times 
The names of the days should be abbreviated as follows: 
Sun. Mon. Tues. Wed. Thurs. Fri. Sat. 
The names of the months should be abbreviated as follows: 
Jan. Feb. Mar. Apr. Aug.  Sept.  Oct. Nov. Dec. 
May, June and July should not be shortened. The abbreviations above should not be used in body text but are reserved for tables, etc., where space is at a premium. 

The forms a.m. and p.m. should be used for times, with a hard space after the figures, e.g. 5.00 p.m. Time zones are abbreviated to initial capitals, e.g. GMT. 

Units of time are symbols, not abbreviations, i.e. s (second), min (minute), 
h (hour) and d (day). They do not take a full stop or an apostrophe when used with numbers greater than one. 
7.2.11 Latin Abbreviations 
A list of the common Latin abbreviations is contained in para. 7.8. Of these, only 
e.g. and i.e. appear frequently. Be careful with their use in documents intended for countries where English is not the main language. In most cases, they can be replaced by for example and that is respectively. 
The use of abbreviations such as q.v. and viz. should be avoided. The use of etc. should not be frequent, as this can suggest a lack of facts or an offhand approach. If a list is preceded by such as, it is incorrect to add etc. The alternative &c. should never be used. 
7.2.12 Measures 
The international symbols for measures should only be used with a number. In all other cases their names should be used, e.g. a milliliter or 1 mL not a mL. 
7.2.13 Names of Countries 
The names of countries should be spelt out in text. Standard abbreviations can be used in tables, captions or other areas where space is at a premium, e.g. UK, USA. 
7.2.14 Names of People 
Initials should be separated by and followed by hard spaces, but periods are not necessary, e.g. J F Kennedy, J-P Sartre. 
7.2.15 Number 
The word number can be represented by no. (plural: nos), being the contraction of the Italian equivalent (numero). A full stop is used to prevent confusion with the word no. An initial capital is not needed unless they are used throughout the expression, e.g. Report No. 2. 
In text, the word—not the contraction—should be used at the beginning of a sentence and in any expression not set off by parentheses, e.g. Number 6 in the series, The most recent issue (No. 4) of the journal… 
A hard space is always used between no., No., nos, Nos and the numeral or numerals. 
7.2.16 Plurals 
Plurals of most shortened forms are formed by adding s without an apostrophe, 
e.g. VDUs, vols, figs, nos. When plurals are indicated by repeating a letter, a period is used after the final letter, e.g. pp. 
7.2.17 Using ‘a’ or ‘an’ 
The use of a or an before a shortened form should be guided by the spoken sound, 
e.g. an RN frigate but a Royal Navy frigate. In this case, the abbreviation RN is spoken letter by letter. Similarly, acronyms are spoken as words, e.g. A UN force… 
7.2.18 Versus 
The word versus should not be abbreviated in text. In tables and figures, the abbreviation vs. may be used. Note this is often written v. in non-US English. 
7.3 NUMBERS 
Numbers can be expressed in figures or words. Figures are preferred but words can look better in descriptive text. There is no absolute rule. Figures should always be used when there is an accompanying symbol or unit of measurement, and in tables. Figures may also be preferable in a sentence with a series of related numbers. The following paragraphs lay out some basic guidelines for the use of numbers. 
7.3.1 Numbers Expressed in Figures 
A comma should be used to separate each group of three figures in numbers greater than 999, e.g. 1,234,567. However, note that some countries, especially those in continental Europe, use a comma instead of a period as a decimal point marker. 
When a decimal number is less than unity, a zero should be placed before the decimal point, e.g. 0.25 not .25. 
An en-dash should be used rather than a hyphen to link spans of figures, e.g. pp. 402–54. An en-dash can be obtained by Ctrl+Num-. 
An en-dash should not be used where numbers have, or could have, negative quantities, e.g. –10 to +55ºC not –10–55ºC. 
No space is need between the figure and symbol or between the symbol and figure in such expressions as 9%, 15ºC, $10m, 5c and 2nd. 
However, a non-breaking space should follow the figure in expressions such as 
10.30 a.m., 9 per cent, 50 ., 100 W. A non-breaking space can be obtained by 
Ctrl+Shift+Space. 
7.3.2 Roman Numerals 
These should not be used unless referring to their use by the customer or to denote plates—see para. 7.10. 
7.3.3 Numbers Expressed as Words 
Numbers less than 100 are generally expressed in words in descriptive text and numbers under ten should always be expressed in words, as this is more readable. 
Numbers over ninety-nine can be shown in words where an approximation is being expressed, e.g. over five hundred aircraft have been sold worldwide. 
Numbers that open a sentence should always be expressed in words. If the number has an associated symbol, e.g. $3m has been invested over a three-year period…, the sentence should be recast, e.g. Over a three-year period, $3m has been… 
A comma should separate two sets of numbers that follow each other, e.g. In 1995, 143 units… 
7.3.4 Fractions 
Hyphens should be used when fractions are expressed as words, e.g. one-third. 
When expressing fractions as figures, only ., ¼, ., ., ½, ., ., ¾ and . are available as existing symbols. Others should be formed by using superscript and subscript, e.g. 11/32” bolt. 
7.3.5 Ordinals 
Words should be used for ordinal numbers, e.g. first, up to and including ninety- nine, and figures thereafter. Words are preferred for round numbers and figures in lists and tables.

7.3.8 Time of Day 
When using the 12-hour clock, the abbreviations a.m. (ante meridiem, before noon) and p.m. (post meridiem, after noon) should be used in lower case with periods. A hard space should be inserted between the figure and the abbreviation,

In either system, figures should always be used. Terms such as ten o’clock or five- forty should not be used. 
7.3.9 Temperature 
Temperature should normally be expressed using the Celsius scale in figures with the degree symbol (º). This symbol is obtained by Ctrl+@ followed by a space. 
Note that there is no space between the figure, the symbol and the letter, e.g. 55ºC or -10ºC. A hard hyphen should be used for negative quantities, obtained by Ctrl+Shift+-. 
When temperature needs to be expressed using the Kelvin scale, no degree symbol is used, and, in common with other SI units, a hard space should be inserted between the figure and the letter, e.g. 5200 K. 
For temperature less than one degree, a zero should be used before the decimal point, e.g. -0.5ºC. 
When the words from and between are used (or implied), ranges of temperatures should be expressed thus: -10 to +55ºC. 
7.3.10 Currency 
The $ and £ currency symbols should precede the figures without spaces. Other currencies should include a hard space, e.g. € 18m or ¥ 15,000. 
Commas should be used to separate each group of three figures. However, note that some countries, especially those in continental Europe, use a comma instead of a full stop as a decimal point marker. 
Where sums of money are expressed in two or more currencies within the same document, each should be qualified, e.g. C$ for Canadian dollars and US$ for US dollars. 
Millions of any currency may be expressed in three ways: 
¦ In full, e.g. $1,000,000 

¦ Using the symbol m with no preceding space, e.g. $2.345m 

¦ Using the word million preceded by a hard space, e.g. € 17 million

7.5 SPELLING 
Unit names only take a plural-s when associated with numbers greater than unity, e.g. 0.5 meter, 1.5 meters. Hertz, lux and siemens are not changed in the plural. Names remain in the singular when used as an adjective, e.g. a 50 ohm load 

Unit names and symbols should not be mixed in the same context, e.g. km/h 
not km/hour 
Per should only be used with words and the solidus (/) should only be used with symbols, e.g. kilometers per hour or km/h 
Symbols do not take periods or take the plural-s when associated with number greater than unity, e.g. 2 kg not 2 kgs 
All prefixes except those representing a million or more, e.g. mega (M), have lower-case symbols 
A zero should be placed before measurements of less than unity, e.g. 0.25 m 
not .25 m 
Unit names and symbols should be separated from associated numbers by a hard space, obtained by Ctrl+Shift+Space, the only exceptions being degree (º), minute (‘), second (“) and degree Celsius (ºC), e.g. 27 m, 27ºC 
The unit should be chosen so that the numerical value of the statement lies between 0.1 and 1000, e.g. 47.32 m not 0.04732 km, 500 kHz or 0.5 MHz not 
500,000 Hz 
Check the spelling in the document before printing, but examine the following: 
I have a spelling checker, It came with my PC. 
It plane lee marks four my revue, Miss steaks aye can knot sea. 
The spell checker does not identify words, which, although correctly spelt, are incorrect in context. For spelling checks, the book or Internet versions of The Merriam-Webster Dictionary or The American Heritage® Dictionary of the English Language should be used where the Spelling function in Microsoft Word is not definitive. Particular points of spelling and usage are described below. 

7.5.1 Official Bodies 
The full names of official bodies should always be spelt exactly as the bodies spell them. For example, the s in Organisation should not be changed to a z. Similarly, pay attention to the use of capitals, hyphens and apostrophes in official names, e.g. International Organization for Standardization. The spelling of non-US organiz- ations should not be changed, e.g. Ministry of Defence. 
7.5.2 Plurals 
Beware of words such as data, media, criteria and phenomena. These are plural nouns but are frequently treated as singular nouns. 
7.5.3 Foreign Geographical Names 
Accepted English forms should be used for foreign place names, e.g. Genoa rather than Genova, or Rome rather than Roma. However, the spelling of place names in other English-speaking countries should not be changed, e.g. Poole Harbour rather than Poole Harbor. 
7.5.4 Word Division 
Word division, i.e. the breaking of a word at the end of a line by a hyphen, is acceptable in fully justified text where horrendous word spacing might occur, or where a one-word last line of a paragraph can be avoided. Whether anything can be gained by word division can be checked by temporarily changing the body text to left flush. Any short lines, followed by breakable words on the following line, will become obvious. The text should be put back to fully justified afterwards by forcing the body text style. 7.6 CAPITALS 
When a word has double consonants, the division should be between the consonants, e.g. admit / tance, but when the two consonants are part of the same root, divide after the second consonant, e.g. pass / ing 
Never break a word before a vowel that forms a syllable, e.g. modu / late 
rather than mod / ulate 
Words with two or more vowels in succession should be divided by sound, 
e.g. ami / able, but letters indicating one sound must not be divided, e.g. 
crea / ture 
Divide a word after a prefix, e.g. proto / type, sub / sequent 
Divide compound words and hyphenated words only between the component parts, e.g. copy / right, self- / addressed 
Never break figures or separate them from associated words, e.g. 50 W not 
50 / W. Never break abbreviations or contractions 
It is preferable not to break a name between initials and surname 
Never break a date expressed entirely in figures. A date expresses in words and figure should only be broken after the month, e.g. 6 July / 1996 not 
6 / July 1996 
Never break the last word of a paragraph. What is the point? 
Proper names are distinguished from common names by the use of an initial capital. Fully capitalized words, i.e. where every letter is in capitals, are only used in headings and, occasionally, to emphasize words (see later). 
7.6.1 Personal Names 
The names of all individuals are always capitalized, e.g. George Washington. However, when personal names have become general words, the initial capital is dropped, e.g. diesel. 
Particles that precede surnames should not have an initial capital, e.g. Leonardo da Vinci. The particle is normally omitted when the first name is omitted, e.g. Otto von Bismarck but Bismarck. An example of when it is not omitted is Charles de Gaulle but de Gaulle. People living in English-speaking countries sometimes capitalize the initial letter of particles in their names, e.g. Van Der Meer, or form one word, e.g. Vandermeer. 
An initial capital is sometimes given to abstract ideas that are personified, usually in poetry. Nicknames and epithets usually have initial capitals, e.g. the Iron Lady. 
7.6.2 Nationalities 
Initial capitals should always be given to names that identify nationalities, etc., 
e.g. American, Christian and Londoner. An initial capital should not be given to a national or geographical name that appears as part of an expression with a specialized meaning, e.g. french fries. 
In the full names of organizations, all components except articles, prepositions and conjunctions are given an initial capital letter, e.g. the United States Air Force. 
There is no rule when the organization is referred to by its generic component and an initial capital is optional, e.g. the Department of Defense but the department, providing that the use is consistent throughout the document. 
7.6.3 Names of Objects 
The names of specific ships, etc., should be given an initial capital and italicized, 
e.g. USS Wasp and HMS Norfolk. The names of brands, models, etc., should just be given an initial capital, e.g. a Harris Falcon II radio. 
7.6.4 Geographical Names 
The official names of specific parts of countries, or cities and towns, always have initial capitals, e.g. California and Los Angeles. 
The names of countries are also always given initial capitals, e.g. the People’s Republic of China and China. 
Initial capitals should be given to names that designate geographical or political groupings, e.g. South-East Asia and Western Europe. Lower case should be used for descriptive and unofficial names, e.g. southern Africa. 
Street names, etc., are always given an initial capital, e.g. MacArthur Court. 
Topographical names are given an initial capital when written in full but not when referred by the generic element, e.g. the Mississippi River but the river. The initial capital is retained, however, when the abbreviated form is commonly used to refer to the place as well, e.g. the Arabian Gulf but the Gulf. The names of public buildings and place are similarly treated, e.g. the Queen Elizabeth II Bridge but the bridge. 
Compass directions are always capitalized (without periods) when abbreviated, 
e.g. ENE, but lower-case when used in full, e.g. east-northeast. 
7.6.5 Historical, Chronological and Ceremonial 
Standard expressions for historical and cultural events are normally given initial capitals but abbreviated references are not, e.g. the First World War but this war. 
Capitals are not required for numerical expressions, e.g. the twentieth century. 
The names of acts, treaties, conventions, protocols, conferences, etc., should receive initial capitals when written in full, e.g. the Treaty of Versailles. Incomplete refer- ences, generic references and plural references should not. 
The names of days of the week and months of the year should have an initial capi- tal but the names of the four seasons should not, e.g. Monday, January, but winter. 
Initial capitals should be used for time zones when spelt out and full capitals when abbreviated, e.g. GMT and Greenwich Mean Time. 
7.6.6 Commercial Terms 
Many trademarks, brand names and proprietary names have become household words, appear to be generic designations for the thing to which they refer, and are written in lower case by those unaware of their origins. However, registered trademarks, brand names, etc., should be written with an initial capital and to print one without may suggest a generic term and thus infringe its trademark status. 
Ideally, trademarks and proprietary names should be avoided and must always be written with an initial capital if they must be used, e.g. Perspex and Velcro. 
Computer terms have to be handled with care, e.g. 
¦ BASIC but Pascal 

¦ UNIX but Microsoft Word 

¦ Apple Macintosh but IBM PC 


7.6.7 Title of Formal Compositions 
The titles of books, etc., should be italicized and written in title case, i.e. maximal capitalization, e.g. Jane’s Defense Weekly. 
The elements of a book should be lowercased except where they begin a sentence, 
e.g. section 10. 
7.6.8 Typographical Uses of Capitals 
Although not recommended, if capitals are to be used for complete words or phrases within the main body of the text, either full capitals or small capitals can be used, providing that the document is consistent. Compare the following examples: 
 
¦ The time for which the receiver stays on a frequency is called the DWELL TIME 

¦ The time for which the receiver stays on a frequency is called the DWELL TIME 




To create small capitals, type the word in lower case first, then select the text, go to Format > Font… and check the Small caps box. 
 
7.7 ITALICS 
 
 
Italics are selected by placing the cursor in the middle of a word (or selecting multiple words) and pressing Ctrl+I. Normal text is called ‘roman’ in printer’s terms. The following rules are applied to the use of italics. 
7.7.1 Titles of Books and Names of Periodicals 
Italics are used for the titles of books and the names of periodicals, etc., e.g. 
Jane’s Military Communications 1995-6, Jane’s Defense Weekly. 
Where the definite article is required to precede the name, it should be written in roman with a lower-case initial letter unless beginning a sentence, e.g. the Los Angeles Times. 
Plural-s and possessive-s should be in roman, e.g. six Daily Heralds and the 
Herald’s editor. Note in the last example that the apostrophe is also roman. 
7.7.2 Names of Ships, Aircraft and other Vehicles 
The names of specific ships, aircraft and other vehicles should be italicized, but not such abbreviations as HMS or USS preceding them, e.g. HMS Norfolk and USS Wasp. 
Where the definite article is required to precede the name it should be written in roman with a lower-case initial letter unless beginning a sentence, e.g. the Southern Cross. The article never forms part of the official name. 
7.7.3 Words and Letters Cited as such 
A word (or words) cited as such is frequently italicized, as is a letter cited as a letter, e.g. The term dwell time is used to describe… 
Alternatively, single quotation marks can be used, especially if the text has other italicized words 
7.7.4 Foreign Words and Phrases 
A foreign word or phrase should only be used if it conveys a sense or nuance that cannot be accurately rendered in English. Such a word or phrase is usually set in italics unless it is considered as anglicized. This is sometimes difficult to determine and no help is generally provided by dictionaries. 
If a word or phrase is italicized, then diacritical marks should be included (and capitalized nouns, if German), e.g. Plus ça change, plus c’est la même chose. Most diacritical marks are available from simple keystrokes—see para. 7.9. 
Diacriticals are often omitted when words or phrases are set in roman. However, they should be retained when omission would alter the pronunciation, e.g. résumé compared with resume.
